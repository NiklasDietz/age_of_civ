# Cross-Monetary-System International Trade: Historical Cases

Research question: in real history, when one country had fiat money and its trade partners did not, what actually happened? Did fiat circulate across borders, or did the civilizations use different media for domestic vs international transactions?

**TL;DR:** Internal fiat + external hard-currency settlement was the **historical norm** from ~960 CE until 1971. Civilizations that issued fiat internally almost always settled cross-border trade in silver, gold, or a foreign hard currency. The "full fiat everywhere" world is less than 55 years old and only works because of USD reserve-currency status.

---

## Documented cases (chronological)

### 1. Song Dynasty China (960–1279) — world's first fiat
- Issued **jiaozi** (1024 CE) and later **huizi** paper money. First government-issued fiat in history.
- **Domestic use:** paper circulated within the empire, convertible to copper coins at imperial exchange offices.
- **International trade:** merchants on the Silk Road and maritime routes paid in **silver ingots (sycee)** or **silk bolts**. Paper did not cross the border. Neighboring states (Xi Xia, Liao, Jin) did not accept Song paper.
- **Lesson:** even the inventor of fiat used metal externally. The paper only worked because imperial authority backed it — foreign merchants had no such enforcement and refused it.

### 2. Yuan Dynasty China (1271–1368) — Kublai Khan's chao
- Kublai issued **Chao** paper notes and banned domestic circulation of gold/silver by decree. First state to attempt full fiat domestically.
- **International trade:** Marco Polo documented that tribute missions and foreign merchants still settled in silver and gold. Chao was not accepted outside Yuan territory.
- **Collapse:** over-issuance during Mongol wars caused hyperinflation; chao became worthless by 1350s.
- **Lesson:** coercive domestic fiat cannot project into international markets. The moment the state weakens, the fiat dies.

### 3. Ming Dynasty — fiat failure, silver takeover (1375–1644)
- Issued **Da Ming Baochao** paper in 1375. Hyperinflated within 70 years.
- By ~1500, **silver became de facto currency** even domestically.
- When Potosí (Bolivia) silver flooded China via Manila galleons (1565–1815), Chinese trade became a global silver sink. European merchants paid **in silver** for tea, silk, porcelain.
- **Lesson:** fiat failure reverts a civilization to commodity money. Silver filled the vacuum.

### 4. Bank of Amsterdam (1609–1819) — dual circulation system
- Bank issued **bank guilders** backed 1:1 by silver held in vaults. Functionally proto-fiat for merchant transactions.
- **Domestic/intra-European wholesale:** bank guilder used extensively — Amsterdam was Europe's clearinghouse.
- **Physical international trade at the harbor:** bulk silver coins (rijksdaalders, Spanish dollars) changed hands.
- Dutch East India Company (VOC) ships carried **physical silver** to Asia, not paper.
- **Lesson:** even a highly-trusted bank-money regime ran a *parallel* specie system for external trade. Two ledgers, two media.

### 5. John Law's Mississippi Scheme — France 1716–1720
- Banque Générale issued fiat **banknotes** backed by shares in the Mississippi Company.
- **Domestic:** notes circulated widely, funded royal debt.
- **International:** Britain, Netherlands refused to accept — their traders demanded specie.
- Bubble burst in 1720 when note-holders demanded gold conversion en masse.
- **Lesson:** foreign trust is the ceiling on fiat adoption. Domestic enforcement cannot compel foreign acceptance.

### 6. American Revolution — Continental dollars (1775–1783)
- Continental Congress issued fiat **Continentals** to fund the war. Famous phrase "not worth a Continental" captures their fate.
- **International:** France and Netherlands extended loans denominated in **livres and guilders** (specie). Continentals were not accepted abroad.
- **Lesson:** wartime fiat almost always collapses. Foreign creditors always demand hard currency.

### 7. Revolutionary France — Assignats (1789–1796)
- Issued to fund revolutionary government, notionally backed by confiscated church lands.
- Hyperinflated ~1000× over 7 years.
- **International trade:** continued in gold and silver. Assignats not accepted across borders.
- **Lesson:** same pattern. Fiat used domestically under compulsion, international trade stays specie.

### 8. **US Civil War — Greenbacks (1862–1879)** — clearest example
This is the cleanest historical case of internal fiat + external specie coexisting in one economy.
- Union issued **Greenbacks** (non-convertible fiat) in 1862 to fund the war.
- Greenbacks traded at a **discount vs gold** domestically: as low as **$2.85 Greenbacks per $1 gold** (July 1864).
- **Customs duties legally had to be paid in gold**, not greenbacks. The government itself refused its own fiat for international trade revenue.
- **Import/export settled in gold.** American exporters received gold; importers paid in gold.
- A **dual price system** operated on the New York markets: "gold prices" (international) and "currency prices" (domestic greenbacks).
- Resumption of specie convertibility at par in 1879 ended the dual system.
- **Lesson:** a country can *legally and practically* run internal fiat + external gold simultaneously for decades.

### 9. Bretton Woods (1944–1971) — fiat-via-gold-proxy
- USD officially convertible to gold at $35/oz (foreign central banks only).
- Other currencies pegged to USD at fixed rates.
- **Mechanism:** everyone used fiat *domestically* but settlement between central banks went through gold or USD-convertible-to-gold. Non-pegged countries (Soviet bloc, China) used their own ruble/yuan internally but paid for Western goods in **gold** or **USD**.
- **Nixon Shock (1971):** Nixon closed the gold window. For the first time in history, the entire world operated on non-convertible fiat.
- **Lesson:** the "full fiat" era is younger than the average civ player. It required a unilateral decision by the reserve-currency issuer and was *not* a coordinated global transition.

### 10. Soviet Bloc (1945–1991) — non-convertible fiat + gold for external trade
- **Domestic ruble:** non-convertible fiat with fixed official prices.
- **Comecon internal trade:** "transferable ruble" — a notional accounting unit, not actual money.
- **Trade with the capitalist world:** settled in **USD, gold, or barter**. USSR sold gold reserves periodically to buy Western grain (especially 1960s–70s).
- **Lesson:** a bloc can run internal fiat while using hard currency for bloc-external trade. Two tiers.

### 11. Modern sanctioned regimes
- **Iran** (post-2012 sanctions): cut off from USD/SWIFT. Trade with Turkey/India settled in **gold** ("oil for gold" revived 2012–2013) and later in rupees/yuan via workarounds.
- **Venezuela** (2016–present): bolivar domestically unusable; foreign trade in USD; military/oil deals with Russia/China in gold or barter.
- **North Korea:** won is domestic-only; international trade in **USD, RMB, gold**, or barter.
- **Zimbabwe** (2008–2009 hyperinflation): ZWD abandoned; foreign AND domestic trade switched to USD/ZAR.
- **Lesson:** weak or untrusted fiat reverts to a hard-currency substitute for cross-border trade. Pattern identical to Yuan/Ming/greenback era — only the hard currency changed from silver to USD.

## Structural pattern across 1000+ years

Every documented case follows the same shape:

1. **Domestic fiat works** only under one of: strong state enforcement, high trust, or compulsion.
2. **International counterparty cannot be compelled.** Foreign merchants/states accept fiat only if they trust the issuer OR have no hard-currency alternative.
3. **Hard currency settlement persists** for cross-border trade regardless of domestic monetary arrangements. Silver → gold → USD → (next?) — the medium changes, the behavior does not.
4. **Parallel systems are normal.** Amsterdam 1609, Civil War USA 1862, USSR 1945, Zimbabwe 2008 — all ran **dual-ledger** economies.
5. **Full-fiat global regime is a historical anomaly**, operative only 1971–present, and it depends on USD reserve-currency status plus an accumulated network effect that is ~50 years old.

## Implications for game design

### Core principle
Treat internal and external settlement as **separate media**. A civ's monetary system is a *domestic* regime. International trade uses the **hardest mutually-accepted medium** available between the two parties.

### Settlement-medium hierarchy
For any trade pair (A, B), settle in the first medium in this list that both accept:

1. Gold bars (universal acceptance)
2. Silver coins (pre-modern universal acceptance)
3. Copper coins (local/regional only)
4. Reserve fiat (only if one civ is designated reserve-currency holder AND partner trusts it)
5. Partner's fiat (only if fiatTrust ≥ threshold; discounted by `(1 − trust)`)
6. Barter (always available, inefficient)

### Two treasuries per civ (or: treasury + commodity hoard split)
- **Domestic treasury:** fiat money, printable (for fiat civs), used for internal maintenance, purchases, wages.
- **External reserves:** gold bars, silver coins, foreign reserve currency. Used to settle cross-border trade, imports, war reparations, bond payments to foreign civs.

Current `MonetaryStateComponent` already tracks `copperCoinReserves`, `silverCoinReserves`, `goldBarReserves` separately from `treasury`. Infrastructure exists — need to route foreign-facing transactions through reserves instead of printable treasury.

### Trade mechanics to add

**Trade routes:**
- Payment settles in hardest mutually-accepted medium.
- Fiat civ trading with commodity civ: fiat civ must *spend gold reserves*, not print money.
- If fiat civ lacks gold reserves, trade route fails or converts at unfavorable rate (buys gold at spot price from market, draining treasury).

**Bond issuance (previous topic):**
- Fiat-issuer to commodity-holder: bond denominated in **gold-equivalent**. Issuer must redeem in gold at maturity. Cannot inflate away.
- Commodity-issuer to fiat-holder: bond denominated in issuer's commodity. Fiat holder treats it as reserve asset.
- Fiat-to-fiat: denominated in issuer's fiat, discounted by trust gap.

**Tribute/reparations/diplomatic deals:**
- Denominated in gold-equivalent unless both parties explicitly agree to fiat settlement.
- Historical precedent: post-WWI reparations on Germany specified in gold marks, not paper marks, precisely to prevent inflation-escape.

### Reserve currency emergence (late-game meta-system)

- First fiat civ to hit `fiatTrust > 0.85` + GDP rank 1 + sufficient network (5+ trade partners) becomes **reserve currency issuer**.
- Reserve status: the reserve issuer's fiat is accepted by all other civs (at small discount) for international settlement, *in place of* gold.
- Enormous economic bonus: reserve issuer can print money to fund imports ("exorbitant privilege" — Giscard d'Estaing).
- Other civs start accumulating reserve fiat in their "external reserves" bucket alongside gold.
- Nixon-shock event: if reserve issuer inflates too much, a *de-peg event* triggers; all holders take a hit on reserve-fiat holdings, reserve status can pass to a rival.

### Cross-system trade-efficiency matrix (simplified)

| Issuer\Partner | Barter | Commodity | GoldStandard | Fiat (high trust) | Fiat (low trust) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Barter**      | 0.50 | 0.55 | 0.55 | 0.55 | 0.50 |
| **Commodity**   | 0.55 | 0.80 | 0.90 | 0.85 | 0.70 |
| **GoldStandard**| 0.55 | 0.90 | 0.95 | 0.95 | 0.75 |
| **Fiat high**   | 0.55 | 0.85 | 0.95 | 1.00 | 0.80 |
| **Fiat low**    | 0.50 | 0.70 | 0.75 | 0.80 | 0.70 |

Applied as a multiplier on route yield. Reflects that high-trust fiat pairs can trade most efficiently (modern world), but fiat-fiat mismatched-trust pairs (Nixon era, Venezuela today) degrade.

### Fiat printing constraints
Currently `printMoney()` increases `treasury` directly. Refinement:
- Printing fills *domestic treasury* only.
- Foreign obligations draw on *gold/silver reserves* first.
- If a fiat civ runs out of reserves and still owes foreign creditors, default cascade triggers (historical: Spain 1557, Mexico 1982, Argentina multiple, Russia 1998). Cannot print away foreign debt.

## Minimum-viable implementation footprint

Priority 1 (single-turn change):
- Route foreign trade-route payments through `goldBarReserves + silverCoinReserves`, not `treasury`, for fiat civs trading with non-fiat civs.

Priority 2 (small addition):
- Cross-system trade-efficiency matrix applied in `TradeRouteSystem::processTradeRoutes()`.

Priority 3 (moderate):
- Bond denomination: `BondIssue::denomination` field (fiat | gold-equivalent). Cross-system bonds default to gold-equivalent. Maturity payment pulls from appropriate treasury/reserve bucket.

Priority 4 (late game, larger):
- Reserve currency emergence event + reserve-status privilege mechanics.
- Nixon-shock-style de-peg event.

All four together reproduce the historical pattern without rewriting the economy.
