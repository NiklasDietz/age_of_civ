# Plate Tectonics — Reference Notes

Comprehensive reference for the continent generator. Covers the core
geophysics that should drive realism in our model.

## 1. Mantle convection (root cause of plate motion)

- Heat from radioactive decay + primordial heat → ~5 TW heat flux through Earth's surface.
- Mantle behaves like a viscoelastic solid: rigid on short timescales, flowing on geologic timescales (millions of years).
- Convection cells ~1000–3000 km wide. Multiple cells per planet, restructuring over ~100 My.
- Hot upwelling at mid-ocean ridges (divergent boundaries).
- Cold downwelling at subduction zones (slab pull).
- Plate driving forces budget:
  - **Slab pull** ~50 % — dense oceanic slab descends into mantle, drags plate.
  - **Ridge push** ~25 % — elevated mid-ocean ridge pushes outward.
  - **Basal drag** ~25 % — viscous coupling with flowing mantle (variable sign).
- **Plumes**: narrow, deep-mantle hot upwellings. Travel through plates as hotspots.

## 2. The plates

- 7 major plates: Eurasian, North American, South American, African, Pacific, Antarctic, Indo-Australian (sometimes split as Indian + Australian).
- 8 minor plates: Nazca, Cocos, Caribbean, Scotia, Arabian, Philippine, Juan de Fuca, Caroline.
- Many microplates (~50 total): Adria, Anatolia, Aegea, etc.
- **Total: ~15 significant plates always present** through the Wilson cycle. Mergers balance rifts.
- **Velocities**: 1–10 cm/yr typical. Pacific Plate ~10 cm/yr (fast — heavily subducting). Eurasian ~1 cm/yr (slow — no slab pull, mostly continental).
- **Mixed crust**: most plates have BOTH continental and oceanic regions. North American Plate has the continent + western half of the Atlantic floor. Eurasian has Eurasia + much of the Arctic. Pacific is almost pure oceanic.

## 3. Continental crust (cratons + accreted terranes)

- **Continental crust**: 30–70 km thick, granitic/metamorphic, density ~2.7 g/cm³, **buoyant** (does not subduct).
- **Cratons**: ancient stable cores, >1 Ga old.
  - **Shields** = exposed Precambrian basement (Canadian, Baltic, African — Kaapvaal, Zimbabwe, West African, Congo, Tanzania).
  - **Platforms** = craton with overlying sediment cover (Russian Platform, US Midwest, Siberian Platform).
- Cratons are essentially "frozen" — no major orogeny in billions of years. Erosion + sediment-burial preserve root rock indefinitely.
- **Continental growth**: cratons act as nuclei. New continental crust accretes onto craton margins via:
  - **Magmatic arc accretion** — volcanic islands collide with continents (Pacific NW Cordilleran terranes, Alaska, Japan->Asia).
  - **Sediment accretion** — eroded mountain debris piles up at craton edges.
  - **Continent-continent suture** — when two continents collide and weld (Ural suture, Iapetus suture).
- Continental rifting cycles can split a craton (Pangaea breakup split the African + South American craton into separate cratons) but the original core blocks each plate piece.

## 4. Oceanic crust (young, dense, recyclable)

- 5–10 km thick, basaltic, density ~3.0 g/cm³, **subducts**.
- Forms at mid-ocean ridges, ages as it spreads outward, eventually subducts at trenches.
- **Maximum age**: ~180 My (any older has subducted). Atlantic floor up to 180 My; Pacific maximum ~165 My.
- **Density increases with age** — older crust is colder, denser, more readily subducts.
- **Bathymetry by age**: ridges at 2.5 km depth; abyssal plains at 5–6 km. Crust subsidence follows √(age).

## 5. Boundary types

| Type | Motion | Examples | Features |
|------|--------|----------|----------|
| **Convergent — ocean/continent** | Closing | Andes, Cascades, Aleutian, Sumatra | Trench, volcanic arc on cont side, accretionary wedge, backarc basin, outer rise |
| **Convergent — ocean/ocean** | Closing | Mariana, Tonga, Philippines | Trench, **volcanic island arc** on overriding plate, slab break-off |
| **Convergent — continent/continent** | Closing | Himalayas, Alps, Urals, Caledonides | Massive crustal thickening, fold-thrust belts, NO subduction, suture zone |
| **Divergent — oceanic** | Spreading | Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East Pacific Rise | Submarine ridge ~2.5 km depth, hydrothermal vents |
| **Divergent — continental** | Spreading | East African Rift, Rio Grande, Baikal | Rift valley, normal-fault graben, basaltic volcanism |
| **Transform** | Sliding | San Andreas, North Anatolian, Alpine NZ, Dead Sea | Strike-slip fault, no orogeny, little volcanism |

## 6. Subduction zone anatomy (most complex boundary)

Cross-section, ocean side → continental side:

1. **Outer rise** (forearc bulge) — slight bathymetric high ~50 km seaward of the trench. Plate flexes UP before plunging.
2. **Trench** — deep narrow depression at the boundary. Mariana Trench ~11 km deep.
3. **Accretionary wedge / forearc** — sediment scraped off the subducting plate piles up against the overriding plate (Olympic Peninsula, Coast Ranges of California).
4. **Forearc basin** — depression between accretionary wedge and arc.
5. **Volcanic arc** — 100–300 km behind the trench, where the slab reaches ~100 km depth and dehydration triggers melting (Andes, Cascades).
6. **Backarc basin** — extensional basin behind the arc, sometimes spreading (Sea of Japan, Lau Basin, Aegean Sea).
7. Wadati-Benioff zone of earthquakes follows the slab down.

## 7. Continental collision (Wilson cycle climax)

When two continents converge:
- Buoyancy prevents subduction.
- Crust crumples and thickens — Tibet has ~70 km thick crust (double normal).
- Mountain belt forms; can rise to 8+ km (Himalayas).
- **Lateral escape**: material squeezed sideways from the collision zone. Indochina pushed SE by India-Eurasia collision.
- **Slab break-off**: the trailing oceanic slab can detach from the buoyant continental crust and sink, causing surface rebound (~1 km uplift across Tibet plateau post-collision).
- **Suturing**: collision welds plates, but the boundary is preserved as a fossil belt with characteristic ophiolite bodies (oceanic crust slivers).

## 8. Wilson cycle (~500 My supercontinent cycle)

Stages:
1. **Initiation** — mantle plume domes continent (East African Rift today).
2. **Rifting** — extension forms graben, valley deepens, sea enters (Red Sea today).
3. **Drifting** — passive margin, ocean basin widens (mature Atlantic).
4. **Subduction** — eventually the basin closes; subduction begins on one or both margins (Pacific today).
5. **Closing** — basin shrinks as ocean is consumed.
6. **Collision** — terminal continent-continent collision (Himalayas).
7. **Suturing** — plates weld; new supercontinent forms.

Earth's known supercontinents:
- **Vaalbara** ~3.6–2.8 Ga (oldest known)
- **Ur** ~3.0 Ga
- **Kenorland** ~2.7 Ga
- **Columbia/Nuna** ~1.8–1.5 Ga
- **Rodinia** ~1.3–0.75 Ga
- **Pannotia** ~600 Ma (brief)
- **Pangaea** 335–175 Ma
- **Future Amasia** ~250 My away (predicted)

## 9. Mountain types and lifespan

| Type | Origin | Lifespan to flat |
|------|--------|------------------|
| Active arc (Andes, Cascades) | Subduction-driven, currently rising | ~50 My active, then erodes ~200 My |
| Active collision (Himalayas) | Continental convergence | Active 50+ My, erodes 300 My |
| Old collision (Appalachians, Urals, Atlas) | Past collision, no current uplift | 250–400 My old, eroded to 1 km |
| Ancient (Caledonides) | Even older collision | ~400 My, ~500 m hills today |
| Hotspot (Hawaii) | Volcanic plume | Each volcano ~5–10 My active, then sinks |
| Rift shoulder (E African) | Active rifting | Lifts during rift, sags after |

**Erosion rates**:
- Active high mountains: 0.5–2 mm/yr
- Mature mountains: 0.1–0.5 mm/yr
- Old mountains: 0.01–0.05 mm/yr (root rock)
- Cratonic interior: <0.01 mm/yr

## 10. Mountain coverage on Earth

- **Mountains** (elevation > 1 km, prominent topography): ~24 % of land area.
- **High mountains** (> 2 km): ~7 % of land area.
- **Hills / low mountains** (0.3–1 km, hilly): ~30 % of land area.
- **Plains / lowlands** (< 300 m, flat): ~35 % of land area.
- **Plateaus**: ~10 % (Tibet, Andean Altiplano, Colorado, Deccan).

Most continental area is FLAT plains (cratonic platforms with sediment cover) or rolling hills (eroded ancient orogens). Mountain ranges are NARROW belts along plate boundaries — Andes ~7000 km long but only ~200–700 km wide.

## 11. Hotspots (mantle plumes)

- Stationary in mantle frame; plates slide over them.
- ~50 active hotspots on Earth: Hawaii, Iceland, Yellowstone, Réunion, Galapagos, Afar, Tristan, Kerguelen, Easter, etc.
- **Hotspot tracks**: chains of progressively older volcanoes. Hawaiian-Emperor chain extends ~6000 km. Each volcano active ~5–10 My then dies as plate carries it off the plume.
- Some plumes can initiate continental rifting if they punch through a continent (Afar — East African Rift; CAMP — Atlantic opening 200 Ma).

## 12. Microplates

- Small fragments between major plates: Caribbean, Cocos, Adria (under Italy), Anatolian, Aegean, Caroline, Burma, Scotia.
- Often capture features from collision/rifting events.
- Move erratically due to multiple boundary forces.

## 13. Other minor processes

- **Glacial isostatic rebound**: post-glacial uplift (Scandinavia, Hudson Bay rising ~1 cm/yr).
- **Polar wandering** (True Polar Wander): the entire crust slowly rotates relative to Earth's rotational axis. ~10° over 100 My in some intervals.
- **Continental drift speed coupling**: faster plates slow during collision (energy → orogeny). India was 15 cm/yr before hitting Eurasia, dropped to 5 cm/yr after collision started.
- **Sediment cycling**: weathering → rivers → coastal/oceanic deposition → eventual subduction or accretion.
- **Volcanic Large Igneous Provinces (LIPs)**: massive flood-basalt events. Deccan ~65 Ma (~2 M km²), Siberian ~250 Ma (~7 M km²), Columbia River ~17 Ma. Causally linked to mass extinctions.

## 14. Key quantitative targets for our sim

- ~15 plates total at any sim time (mergers ≈ rifts).
- ~10 % continental area = mountain.
- ~25 % continental area = hills.
- Most coastlines are PASSIVE margins (no orogeny). Active margins are rare.
- Mountain belts are NARROW (~200–700 km wide) and LONG (~5000 km).
- Cratonic interiors are FLAT and stable for billions of years.
- Mountain elevation rises from 0 → peak over ~30–80 My, then erodes for ~200–400 My.
